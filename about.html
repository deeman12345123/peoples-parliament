The Experiment

What if we ran
the country?

The People's Parliament is a long-term civic experiment to find out whether ordinary Australians, given a genuine vote on real policy issues, would govern better than the politicians they elected to do it for them.

If every Australian could vote directly on every major policy decision — not once every three years, but every single time — would we make better choices than the government we elect to represent us?

The central question of this experiment
How It Works
01
Real Issues, Real Votes

We take actual policy questions facing Australia — housing, health, defence spending, environmental law — and put them to a direct public vote. No political spin. Just the question and your answer.

02
Track the Real Government

When the real government makes a decision on the same issue, we record it on the Scoreboard. Then the public rates both decisions — ours and Canberra's — for satisfaction and effectiveness.

03
The Experiment Runs

Over months and years, the data accumulates. We build a picture of what Australians actually want, how it differs from what they get, and whether direct democracy produces better outcomes.

Our Principles
⚖️
Strictly Non-Partisan

We do not advocate for any political party, ideology, or predetermined outcome. Polls are written to be neutral. The data goes where the data goes. We follow the votes, not a platform.

🔍
Transparent by Design

Every poll result is published in full. Every scoreboard comparison is sourced. Every methodology decision is documented. Nothing is hidden, cherry-picked, or adjusted after the fact.

🇦🇺
Australian Voices Only

Voting is restricted to Australian IP addresses. This is a national experiment about Australians governing Australia — not a global poll. Every voice here is a voice from this country.

📊
Long-Term Thinking

This isn't a news cycle stunt. The experiment runs indefinitely. The value of the data compounds over time — the longer it runs, the more meaningful the comparison becomes.

What we're really testing

The common argument against direct democracy is that ordinary people can't be trusted to make complex policy decisions — that they're too uninformed, too emotional, too susceptible to populism.

The common argument against representative democracy is that politicians stop representing the people the moment they're elected — captured by donors, party machinery, and self-interest.

This experiment takes both arguments seriously. We're not trying to prove one side right. We're gathering real data on real decisions, over real time, to see what actually happens when Australians are given the chance to govern directly.

After five years, ten years, twenty years — what does the record show? That's the question this project is designed to answer.

What We Measure

🗳️
Vote Outcomes
What policy did Australians choose, and by what margin? How close or divided was the result?
🏛️
Government Comparison
What did Canberra actually do on the same issue? Did it align with what the people voted for?
📈
Satisfaction Ratings
After each decision, the public rates both outcomes. Who got the better result — us or them?
💰
Budget Priorities
How do Australians allocate $650B versus how Canberra actually spends it? Where are the gaps?
📅

A cumulative scoreboard tracking every decision over the full life of the experiment.

The Rules of the Experiment

To keep this honest, we follow a strict set of principles for how polls are written, published, and recorded.

✔
Polls are written before outcomes are known We never write a poll question after seeing how the government acted — that would introduce bias toward or against them.
✔
Questions are neutral and factual No leading language. Each poll presents the issue plainly with a brief factual context and a yes/no or multiple choice answer.
✔
Results are never altered Once a poll closes, the result is locked. No votes are removed, adjusted, or reweighted after the fact for any reason.
✔
Scoreboard comparisons are sourced Every "Canberra decision" entry links to the official source — legislation, budget papers, or ministerial announcement.
✔
Voting is IP-restricted to Australia We use Australian IP detection to restrict voting to Australian users. This isn't perfect, but it's the best available method without mandatory ID.
✔
All data is publicly archived Every poll, every result, every scoreboard entry is stored in the public archive. Nothing is deleted, even results we might prefer to forget.
Frequently Asked Questions
Is this affiliated with the Australian Government or any political party?
No. The People's Parliament is a completely independent civic experiment. We have no affiliation with any government, political party, lobby group, or media organisation. We do not accept advertising or sponsored content. This project is run by volunteers who believe in civic engagement.
How do you prevent people voting multiple times?
Currently we restrict voting to one vote per IP address per poll. We're aware this isn't foolproof — households share IPs, VPNs exist. We're transparent about this limitation. As the project grows, we're exploring more robust verification. For now, the volume of genuine votes is large enough that manipulation would require a coordinated and significant effort.
The decisions here are symbolic — do they actually matter?
The decisions are symbolic in that they don't have legal force. But that doesn't make the data meaningless. Over time, a consistent record of what Australians actually want — versus what they get — becomes a powerful piece of civic evidence. Journalists, researchers, and citizens can use this data to ask hard questions of the government. Democracy doesn't only happen in parliaments.
Who writes the poll questions, and how do you ensure they're neutral?
Poll questions are drafted by our volunteer editorial team, based on current issues reported in Australian news media. Every question is reviewed by at least two people before publishing. We aim for factual, plain-language framing. If the community believes a question is biased, they can flag it via the contact page and we'll review it publicly.
How is the scoreboard comparison determined?
When the real government acts on a policy we've already voted on, we record their decision and link to the primary source. We then run a satisfaction poll asking the public to rate both the people's decision and the government's decision. The satisfaction score — not our editorial opinion — determines the winner. We don't choose who wins. The public does, after the fact.
Can I propose new legislation for the shadow parliament to vote on?
Yes. Go to the Active Polls page and scroll to the "Propose Legislation" section. Any Australian can submit a policy idea. Proposals with strong community support are reviewed by our editorial team and, if appropriate, put to a formal vote. We aim to run proposals from across the political spectrum.
