🇳🇴 The Norway Question — Australia's Biggest Missed Opportunity

Should every Australian receive a share of mining profits — like Norway does?

🇳🇴 What Norway Did
AU$655,000
per Norwegian citizen — saved in their sovereign wealth fund
Norway discovered oil in 1969. Instead of letting companies pocket all the profits, the government put petroleum revenues into a national fund — now worth US$2.2 trillion — the largest sovereign wealth fund on Earth. Every Norwegian effectively owns AU$655,000 of it.
🇦🇺 What Australia Did
$0
per Australian citizen saved from mining wealth
Australia has one of the richest mineral deposits on Earth — iron ore, coal, gas, gold, lithium. The mining industry earns AU$492 billion a year. Yet 86% of Australian mining is foreign-owned — meaning most of those profits leave the country entirely. Australians get almost nothing from their own ground.
The numbers in plain English: Australian mining generates roughly AU$153 billion in profit every year. Of that, AU$131 billion leaves Australia — going to foreign shareholders. State governments collect about AU$18 billion in royalties — less than 4 cents in every dollar of revenue. The federal government collects about AU$42 billion in company tax — but mining companies use offsets, deductions, and transfer pricing to reduce this significantly.

If Australia had started a Norway-style fund in 1990 and saved just 5% of mining export revenues since then, the fund today would be worth roughly AU$1 trillion — or about AU$37,000 per Australian.

Norway's secret? They didn't let the mining companies own the resources. The resources belong to the Norwegian people. They just hire companies to extract them. Australia does the opposite — it gives companies the resources almost for free, takes a small royalty, and waves goodbye to the profits.

Is "Ditch the Witch" sexist — and should it be allowed?

Happening right now in Melbourne: A billboard truck has been driving around Melbourne's CBD showing Victorian Premier Jacinta Allan wearing a witch's hat with the slogan "Ditch the Witch." The $105,000 campaign was funded by a local brothel owner and other Melbourne business owners who say the slogan is not sexist.

The background: "Ditch the Witch" was first used in 2011 against Julia Gillard — Australia's first female Prime Minister — at a rally where Tony Abbott spoke without objecting to the signs. Gillard responded with her famous 2012 "misogyny speech" in parliament, which went viral worldwide and is now studied in schools.

Now in June 2026, the same slogan is back — this time aimed at Victorian Premier Jacinta Allan. Both Gillard and PM Anthony Albanese have publicly condemned it. The billboards' funder says it is legitimate political protest.

Two real questions here — you decide:
1. Is the language sexist — targeting these women because they are women?
2. Even if it is sexist — should it be allowed in a democracy?
